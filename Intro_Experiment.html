What is the purpose of the study?

We are interested in how people make decisions about foraging, exploring and resting in different kinds of environment, and about how such decision-making might relate to mood. We have developed an online task which is designed to examine these questions.

Who is organising and sponsoring this research?

Cambridge University is the sponsor for this study. We will be using information from you in order to undertake this study and will act as the data controller for this. Your rights to access, change or move your information are limited, as we need to manage your information in specific ways in order for the research to be reliable and accurate. If you withdraw from the study, we will destroy any data about you that we have already obtained. To safeguard your rights, we will use the minimum personally-identifiable information possible. You can find out more about how we use your information at:

https://www.medschl.cam.ac.uk/research/privacy-notice-how-we-use-your-research-data/

Has the study been reviewed?

This study has been reviewed by an independent group of people (Cambridge Psychology Research Ethics Committee) who have a duty to protect research volunteers' safety, rights, wellbeing and dignity.

Why have I been invited to take part?

Do I have to take part?

It is up to you whether or not you decide to take part. Before deciding you should read this information sheet and ask about anything that is not clear. If you decide to take part we will ask you to fill in a form indicating that you have agreed (consent form). However, even after you have completed that form you can withdraw from the study without having to give us a reason.

What will I be asked to do in the study?

If you decide to take part, we will ask you to complete an online experimental task measuring resource conservation and foraging. We will ask you to use the arrow keys on the computer keyboard to search for "energy points" in a series of environments pictured on the computer screen. Your task will be to collect as many points as possible while limiting how far you have to go to find them. We will also ask you to fill out some questionnaires about various aspects of your mood and report on previous mental health diagnoses you might have received.

How long will it take?

We expect the session to take up to 45 minutes, including plenty of time to allow for rests between tasks.

Will I find the study upsetting?

Some of the questionnaire items will ask you about symptoms of low mood and anxiety, including any past episodes of depression or times when you have been very anxious. If thinking or talking about these topics causes you to feel any distress, there is no need to continue with the study. We have provided information about relevant services and helplines at the bottom of this page.

You may withdraw from the study at any time, without explaining why. You can withdraw by pressing the "Cancel" button during the session. However, once you have completed the experiment, your data will not be able to be deleted since we would be unable to identify which data belongs to you.

What happens to my data?

Your research data, typically combined with similar information from other volunteers, will be used for scientific purposes. The results are typically presented in scientific papers and talks, in teaching and in explaining our science to health professionals, school groups and the public in general. We take great care to ensure that individuals cannot be identified from our research outputs.

Undertaking scientific studies is expensive and relies on the generous contribution of time from volunteers. To make the most of your research data, we plan to look after it for the long term and may use it to answer research questions beyond those for which it was originally collected. This may include combining research data from this study with research data from other studies in the CBU in which you have been involved. In addition to our own analyses, we agree with the principle that research data, often collected with public money, are a public good, produced in the public interest, which should be made openly available with as few restrictions as possible in a timely and responsible manner. Many of the bodies that fund our research insist that we follow this principle. In line with this, we may also share research data with other researchers in the UK and around the world, free of charge. This is important for the scientific process, so that other researchers have the opportunity to check our results, or conduct new analyses. It also helps avoid research duplication.

We are aware that, very rarely, research data could be used to identify an individual (for example, questionnaire responses about life events could identify a particular person to someone who knows him or her). In such cases, we take great care to reduce the chances of such identification by omitting critical details. Moreover, in most cases, we will require people who access research data to agree to a "data usage agreement", where they agree not to try to identify anyone, and not share the data will anyone else. However, on occasion, there is a need to make the data fully "open". If research data are made open, then we have no control over how that information is used.

You should only agree to participate in this study if you have understood and consent to this re-use and sharing of research data.

If you change your mind and later withdraw your consent to participate in this study (you can contact us via email anna.bevan@mrc-cbu.cam.ac.uk or a Prolific message), we will not collect any additional data about you. We will delete your data if you withdraw before the study was published or shared with others. However, any research data already published or shared with other investigators cannot be destroyed, withdrawn or recalled.

Instructions

Following this page, you will be asked to complete an experimental task. You will be shown a picture of a map with different elements such as trees, mountains, and rivers — like the image below:

Scattered throughout the map, there are hidden "energy points" that provide you energy when you touch them. Every time a new environment is generated, you will be asked to do a couple of things: First, you will be asked to rate your mood and energy at the moment. Then, you will be shown the environment for 2 seconds, and then asked to estimate how many energy points are available in the environment, and how certain you are in your prediction. Then, you will be given a choice to "Forage" or to "Rest".

If you decide to forage, you will be able to move through the map using the arrow keys, and attain energy points. However, every time you take a step, you lose one energy point. As you spend and gain more energy points, a counter at the bottom of your screen will indicate how many points you have. You can explore for as long or as little as you like, as long as you have energy points available. Once you are done with exploring, you can press the "F" key to go ahead to the next environment.

If you choose to rest, you will be unable to move on the map for some time, and the next environment will be generated once your waiting time is done. However, during this time, you will gain a consistent amount of energy points.

There are 18 environments in total, and breaks are allocated every 3 environments in case you need them. When you are done, you will be asked to complete some questionnaires. Once you feel like you understand the instructions, please click the button to start the task!

This experiment was made to be done on computer, and can run on any major browser application. However, we recommend that you use either Google Chrome or Microsoft Edge for greater support. Depending on the size of your screen, the task elements may appear too large or too little. Therefore, when completing the experiment, please adjust your screen zoom using Ctrl +/- until all elements comfortably fit on your screen.
